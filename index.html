Optimizing and Predicting Emergency Calls

Let's Go!

Deliverables

The goal of this website is to present a solution to the following problem: How can we optimize and predict emergency call dispatches in San Francisco? This website displays visuals of four interesting metrics of the given data, including a heat map, and answers the questions, "Which dispatch is the most likely to be required for a given emergency?" and "Which areas take the longest time for dispatched vehicles to reach?" The goal of this website is to not only prvoide an answer to these questions, but present them in a fashion that is responsive, simple to navigate, and, above all, beautiful.

Areas that Take the Longest Time to Dispatch

And How This Can Be Reduced

The Answer is Rather Unsurprising

The areas that take the longest time for dispatch vehicles to reach are areas that are the furthest away from main dispatch centers. This is true for absolute distance from the dispatch center to the area in need (the distance as the crow flies) and is also true for distance in road miles as well. A majority of major roads in San Francisco are one-way, meaning it can be difficult to reach certain spots within the city.

However, These Problems Can Be Solved

One of the ways this problem can be solved is to have more, small-scale dispatch centers around the city, especially in places that are not well received by current centers. This does not have to mean building full-scale dispatch centers in these areas; it can be as simple as having a number of emergency service vehicles and first-respondents on street corners, ready to be deployed if need be before backup from larger dispatch centers can help.

Preparing for the Future

Problem: A Rising Increase in Dispatch Calls

There are a number of areas that are experiencing an increase in dispatch calls around San Francisco. These areas tend to be where the average income is lower than neighboring areas combined with a relatively large population in a small amount of area. These calls range from low priority calls, to emergency calls that require an ambulance directed to the hospital. In my opinion, the latter are the most important types of calls that are experiencing an increase and should be given the most attention in working towards building a solution to decrease the rise in emergency calls.

Solution: Wealth Center in Impacted Areas

As I see it, it is rather unreasonable to build entirely new dispatch centers or hospital to reach areas that are experiencing an increase of emergency medical calls. Even though it would help the community greatly, the cost of making land for and building such centers would outweigh the benefits it may provide. Instead, as a compromise, smaller "Wealth Centers" could be built instead. Thees do not need to take up nearly as much space or cost as a regular hospital, but it can still provide ambulances and first respondent vehicles to help combat the rise in medical-related dispatch calls.
